Simplicity is not the absence of effort — it is the result of it.

Anyone can add. Removing things requires conviction. Knowing what to leave out means understanding what matters, and that understanding is rarely free.

## The instinct to add

When something isn't working, the first impulse is almost always to add something. More features, more words, more steps. Complexity accumulates easily, invisibly, until the thing you built can no longer be understood — even by you.

The better instinct, harder to develop, is to ask: *what can be removed without losing anything essential?*

## Constraints as a tool

Some of the best work is produced under constraints. A word limit forces precision. A single-purpose tool does its job better than a general one. A sparse room is easier to think in than a cluttered one.

Constraints aren't limitations — they're clarity.
